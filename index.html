§ 01 — The Pattern

Consider the scale of it. Every meeting that sent an organisation in the wrong direction because the decision appeared shared when it was not. Every restructure built on a diagnosis that was never actually agreed upon. Every generation taught about something rather than through something — and called it education.

The pattern is not dramatic. It does not announce itself. It operates precisely in the space where things look fine — where the meeting concluded well, where everyone nodded, where the document was signed.

Apparent alignment diverging silently from actual alignment. The gap between what was said and what was understood. The difference between the map and the territory — when neither party knows they're reading a different map.

This is not a communication failure in the conventional sense. Better communication tools do not fix it. More information does not fix it. The problem is not volume. It is a calibration failure at the level of meaning itself — and it compounds across every decision that follows.

Think of what that compounding looks like across thousands of years of human coordination. The wasted cognition is not a rounding error. It may be one of the largest drains on human potential ever to go unnamed.

§ 02 — Origin

For most of my life, I found myself on the edge of systems rather than comfortably inside them. At the time, I thought this was a personal story. Over time, a different question emerged.

Why do intelligent people repeatedly misunderstand one another even when they appear to agree? Why do organisations fail despite talented people, abundant information, and apparent alignment? Why do teams, institutions, and even societies spend so much energy correcting problems that nobody intended to create?

What began as a personal inquiry gradually became a systems inquiry. Over several years I documented observations across decision-making, communication, organisational behaviour, cognitive science, AI, and coordination theory. Again and again I encountered the same pattern.

The most damaging failures were often not caused by disagreement. They occurred when disagreement remained hidden beneath the appearance of agreement. Teams believed they were aligned when they were not. Organisations believed information had been shared when it had not. People believed they understood each other when they were operating from entirely different models.

I am not interested in helping people think harder.
I am interested in helping people waste less cognition
on avoidable misunderstanding.

Because if a significant portion of human effort is currently spent compensating for invisible coordination failures, then improving our ability to see those failures may represent one of the highest-leverage opportunities available.

And the solution may be older than any institution currently teaching the opposite. Learn by doing is not a pedagogical preference. It is a statement about how understanding actually forms. You do not understand and then act. You act, and understanding emerges from the friction. Education built its entire architecture on a false model of how cognition works. The new age of intelligence demands a correction.

§ 03 — The Formalisation
Aₐ − Aₘ = ΔE
Aₐ
Apparent alignment. What looks agreed. What the meeting concluded. What the document confirmed. What the nod communicated.
Aₘ
Actual model alignment. What is genuinely understood the same way. What would survive a precise test of shared meaning.
ΔE
The delta. The gap that compounds invisibly until it surfaces at execution — by which point the cost is already sunk.

This is not a new observation. Jerry Harvey named it behaviourally in 1974. Cannon-Bowers and Salas identified shared mental model divergence as a primary cause of team failure in 1993. Diane Vaughan showed it operating at institutional scale across time. Stuart Russell identified its precise equivalent at the human-AI interface.

The pattern appears across every domain. We suggest the issue could be corrected — upstream.

Collective
Intelligence
Superminds cannot compound collective intelligence if the alignment between participating agents — human and machine — is apparent rather than actual. The Aₐ − Aₘ gap is what prevents genuine collective intelligence from emerging.
Constructive
Communication
Communication tools operate at the social and discourse layer. Apparency Error operates at the layer beneath — shaping whether communication achieves shared understanding or improves the quality of exchanges between people whose models are already diverging invisibly.
Dialogue &
Coherence
Bohm's contribution: incoherence is systemic. Thought participates in what it perceives. Third Intelligence builds infrastructure for coherent collective sensemaking — not persuading people what to believe, but making divergence visible while it is still correctable.
